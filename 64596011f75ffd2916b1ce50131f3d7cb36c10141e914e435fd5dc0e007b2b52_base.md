# Developmental toxicity of sodium fluoride measured during multiple generations

T.F.X. Collins\*, R.L. Sprando, T.N. Black, M.E. Shackelford, N. Olejnik, M.J. Ames,
J.I. Rorie, D.I. Ruggles

Center for Food Safety and Applied Nutrition, US Food and Drug Administration, 8301 Muirkirk Road, Laurel, MD 20708, USA

Accepted 9 November 2000

## Abstract

Sodium fluoride (NaF) has been used to fluoridate drinking water in the United States since the mid 1940s. Because of the lack of
reliable studies on the multigeneration effects of the compound, NaF (0, 25, 100, 175 or 250 ppm in drinking water) was given to
rats continuously during three generations. Parental (F0) generation rats were treated for 10 weeks and mated within groups. At
gestation day 20, caesarean sections were performed and eight F0 females per group and their litters (F1) were observed for implant
status, fetal weight and length, sex and morphological development. The remaining F0 females (29-32 per group) were allowed to
litter. F1 offspring (36 of each sex per group) were mated within groups, and caesarean sections were performed at gestation day 20.
The F1 females and their litters (F2) were observed for implant status, fetal weight and length, sex and morphological development.
In addition, F2 fetuses were evaluated for internal (soft-tissue) and skeletal development. Decreased fluid consumption for F0 and
F1 dams at 175 and 250 ppm was attributed to decreased palatability of the solution. No dose-related effects in feed consumption or
mean body weight gain were observed in either F0 or F1 females. Numbers of corpora lutea, implants, viable fetuses and fetal
morphological development were similar in all groups. No dose-related anomalies in internal organs were observed in F2 fetuses.
Ossification of the hyoid bone of F2 fetuses was significantly decreased at 250 ppm. Because of the decreased ossification of the
hyoid bone, 250 ppm is considered the effect level. Published by Elsevier Science Ltd.

Keywords: Sodium fluoride; Reproduction; Developmental toxicity; Rat

## 1. Introduction

In an effort to reduce the incidence of caries in the
American population, fluoride has been added to tap
water since the mid-1940s. Sodium fluoride was the first
fluoride compound used in the fluoridation of drinking
water, and it is still commonly used for that purpose.

When the levels of fluoride in drinking water were
tested and set, water was the only measurable source of
fluoride for most communities. Now, in addition to
drinking water, adults, children and pregnant females
ingest fluoride with foods and beverages prepared with
fluoridated water, and they are exposed to fluoride-con-
taining dental products. As a result, there is greater expo-
sure to fluoride than had been anticipated. Transplacental
passage of fluoride in humans and animals has been shown

(Zipkin and Babeaux, 1965; Teotia et al., 1979; Ron et al.,
1986; Caldera et al., 1987; Forestier et al., 1990), and
incorporation into fetal tissues after passage through the
placenta has also been shown (Theuer et al., 1971).

Chronic toxicity studies lasting up to 2 yr were done
in rats and mice by the National Toxicology Program
(1990) to test the possible carcinogenic activity sodium
fluoride. NaF (0, 25, 100 or 175 ppm in drinking water)
was given to rats during the 2-yr study. At the end of
the 2-yr period, no evidence of carcinogenic activity was
observed in female rats, but there was equivocal evi-
dence of carcinogenic activity in male rats, based on the
occurrence of a small number of osteosarcomas of bone
in male rats given 100 and 175 ppm. Equivocal evidence
is a category for uncertain findings from studies that
show a marginal increase in neoplasms that may be
related to chemical administration.

In the early 1990s, existing NaF studies were reviewed
in several reports, and studies of reproductive effects
were considered inadequate to determine potential
reproductive or developmental hazards. These reports

Since the above-mentioned reports were issued in the
early 1990s, several single-generation studies using cur-

animals have been done on the effects of fluoride on the
in utero development of rats (Collins et al., 1995; Hein-
del et al., 1996) and rabbits (Heindel et al., 1996). This
study was done to determine any generational, possibly
cumulative, effects on the development of the offspring
when sodium fluoride was ingested continuously
through multiple generations. The study was designed
to simulate human exposure in utero when both parents
have been exposed to the compound before and during
mating and continue to be exposed during gestation.

## 2. Materials and methods

### 2.1. Test material

Sodium fluoride (NaF) (CAS no. 7681-49-4, Sigma
Chemical Co., St Louis, MO, USA; Lot no. 109F0102)
was as pure as the standard reference sample purchased
from the US Pharmacopeia (Rockville, MD, USA).
Both samples were analyzed at the Food and Drug
Administration for Na and F concentrations and trace
element contaminants. Compound analysis was descri-
bed in Collins et al. (2001). Weight/volume NaF solu-
tions were prepared in water obtained by filtering
house-distilled water through a Hydro Pico pure water
system (Research Triangle Park, NC, USA). The con-
centration of fluoride in the Pico system treated water
was less than 0.2 ppm. Sodium fluoride concentrations
for both the control and treated groups were determined
each time dosing solutions were prepared for any group.
The method of determining the concentration of the
solutions was described previously (Collins et al., 2001).
The following NaF solutions were used for this study: 0
(Pico system water only), 25, 100, 175 or 250 ppm. The
25, 100 and 175 ppm doses were based on the National
Toxicology Program (1990) chronic 2-yr study. The 250
ppm dose was based on the developmental toxicity
study conducted in our laboratory (Collins et al., 1995).

### 2.2. Test animals and animal husbandry

Caesarean-derived (CD CRL:CD-BR), viral anti-
body-free rats were obtained from Charles River

Laboratories, Inc. (Wilmington, MA, USA). The males
and females weighed 51-75 g at the time of receipt. The
animals were uniquely identified by ear tags.

Throughout the study, males and females were fed
low-fluoride NIH-07 diet (7.95 ppm fluoride) to mini-
mize interference with fluoride in the drinking water.
The diet was prepared by Ziegler Bros., Inc. (Gardners,
PA, USA), the company that provided the low-fluoride
diet for the National Toxicology Program (1990) stud-
ies, and the diet was the same formulation as that used
in the NTP studies.

Single animals were housed in stainless-steel cages
suspended in stainless-steel racks. Pregnant females
were housed in polycarbonate tubs with Sani-Chips (P.
J. Murphy Forest Products Corp., Montville, NJ,
USA). In the study room, light was provided on a 12-h
light/dark cycle, the average temperature was 71-73ºF,
and the average humidity was 35-67%. Fluid con-
sumption was measured every 3 days and feed con-
sumption was measured every 7 days. Each animal was
individually examined for signs of toxicity on a daily
basis throughout the study. Because the diet was in meal
form and the animals could not gnaw at their food, their
incisors were routinely trimmed to prevent malocclu-
sion, a condition that could jeopardize the animals'
ability to eat.

### 2.3. Experimental design

The effects of NaF on developmental toxicity mea-
sured during the parental (F0) generation and two filial
(F1 and F2) generations are reported here. The repro-
ductive and postnatal growth aspects of the study were
reported in Collins et al. (2001), and the effects on male
reproduction were reported by Sprando et al. (1997, 1998).

This study was done according to Good Laboratory
Practice regulations. After an acclimatization period of
1 wk, each F0 animal was assigned to the control or one
of the four treatment groups by stratified random pro-
cedure. Each F0 group contained 48 males and 48
females. The animals were treated for 10 wk, then
mated 1:1 within each group. Mating was confirmed by
the presence of sperm in the vaginal lavage. Females
that had mated were presumed pregnant. The day on
which mating was confirmed was designated as the
female's gestation day 0.

### 2.4. Caesarean sections of F0 animals on day 20

On gestation day 20, caesarean sections were per-
formed on eight mated F0 females per group, and
reproductive and developmental parameters were mea-
sured. The mated F0 females were observed for the last
time, euthanized in an atmosphere of carbon dioxide,
and caesarean sections were performed in the sequence
in which the animals were assigned to the study. Any

anomaly in the maternal organism was recorded. The
uterus of each animal was opened and examined in situ
for the presence and position of resorption sites,
implantation sites, and live or dead fetuses. Deciduomas
(brownish implantation sites without placentas) were
called early deaths, and implantation sites with pla-
centas and with complete but non-viable fetuses that
were of subnormal size, that showed retarded develop-
ment, or that were in a macerated condition were clas-
sed as late deaths, according to the glossary developed
by the Mid-Atlantic Reproduction and Teratology
Association (MARTA) (1986). The uteri of animals that
did not appear pregnant were stained with sodium sul-
fide to enhance the observation of resorption sites. The
numbers of corpora lutea were counted and recorded.

Each fetus was removed from the uterus and the
umbilical artery was crimped and severed. Each viable
fetus was sexed, weighed, measured for crown-rump
length, and examined under magnification for any
deviations from normality. Sex was determined by the
distance between the genital papilla and the anal open-
ing. After the developmental parameters of these eight
F0 females and their fetuses had been recorded, the
animals were given to another investigator for a sepa-
rate study. The remaining F0 females were allowed to
litter and wean their pups. On postnatal day 21, 36 F1
males and 36 F1 females per group were randomly
selected to provide the F2 generation. The F1 animals
were given low-fluoride diet and control or fluoridated
water for 10 wk, then mated (1:1) within groups.

2.5. Caesarean sections of F1 animals on day 20 and
fetal evaluations of F2 fetuses

At gestation day 20, the mated F1 females were
observed for the last time, euthanized, and caesarean
sections were performed in the manner described above
for F0 females. The viable fetuses were euthanized and
alternately placed in vials containing 95% ethanol for
the evaluation of skeletal abnormalities or Bouin's
solution for the evaluation of soft-tissue abnormalities.
After fixation and dehydration in 95% ethanol, the
fetuses were macerated in potassium hydroxide and
stained with Alizarin Red S (Dawson, 1926). Fetal
skeletons were examined for deviations from normality
in the sternebrae, ribs, vertebrae, dorsal arches, skull,
and appendicular skeleton. The fetuses fixed in Bouin's
solution were serially sectioned by razor blade and the
internal (soft tissues) were examined (Wilson, 1965).
Calculation of runts was done in the manner described
in Collins et al. (2001).

### 2.6. Control of bias

Based on random procedures, each sperm-positive
female was assigned a test number which was used as a

litter number after euthanasia of the dam. All evalua-
tions of the dams and fetuses during caesarean sections,
and during skeletal or visceral analysis, were done
without knowing the dose level to which the animals
belonged to preclude the possibility of bias.

### 2.7. Statistical analysis

The data for treated and control animals were ana-
lyzed by the Division of Mathematics, CFSAN, US
Food and Drug Administration. Differences between
treated groups and the control groups that generated P
values equal to or below 0.05 (P<0.05) were considered
statistically significant. For feed consumption, number
of corpora lutea, implants, total alive, male and female
alive, an analysis of variance (ANOVA) was performed.
If the ANOVA indicated a significant effect, then a least
significant difference (LSD) test was run to compare the
control with each treated group. For implantation effi-
ciency and early, late and total (early + late) resorptions,
the data were first transformed using a Freeman-Tukey
arcsine transformation (Freeman and Tukey, 1950) fol-
lowed by an ANOVA. If the ANOVA indicated a sig-
nificant effect, then a LSD test was performed as
discussed above. For body weight gain and gravid uter-
ine weight, an analysis of covariance (ANCOVA) was
performed. If the ANCOVA showed a significant effect,
a LSD test was run to compare the control with each
treated group. For fetal weight and crown-rump mea-
surements, a nested ANOVA followed by a LSD test
was performed on the groups if the nested ANOVA
showed a significant effect. For the incidences of clinical
signs in male and female rats, and specific soft-tissue,
sternebral, and skeletal variations in fetuses, a Fisher's
exact test was used to compare each treated group to the
control group. The same test was used to compare the
control with each treated group for the number of litters
with at least one, at least two, and at least three soft-
tissue, sternebral and skeletal variations. For the aver-
age number of fetuses per litter with at least one, at least
two, and at least three soft-tissue, sternebral or skeletal
variations, the data were first transformed by a Free-
man-Tukey arcsine transformation (Freeman and
Tukey, 1950). The transformed data were then analyzed
using an ANOVA followed by a LSD test as explained
above. For the average number of ossified vertebrae in
fetuses, a nested ANOVA was performed on the data. If
the nested analysis was significant, a LSD test was
performed to compare the control with each treated
group.

Several animals (distributed throughout all groups)
played with their water tubes, causing the fluid to drip.
This led to unusually and misleadingly high fluid con-
sumption values for these animals. For statistical ana-
lysis of mean fluid consumption, each dose group was
first checked for outliers (i.e. animals with very high

## 3. Results

### 3.1. Clinical findings and morphological development

Clinical findings were unremarkable during gestation
of F0 and F1 females. The death of a single female in
the 250 ppm F1 group was not compound related.
Morphological development of F1 and F2 fetuses was
similar in all groups.

### 3.2. Findings during gestation

Overall fluid consumption showed dose-related
decreases at 100, 175 and 250 ppm, and the decreases
were statistically significant at 250 ppm in F0 dams and
175 and 250 ppm in F1 dams (Table 1). The decreases
corresponded to decreased palatability of the solution.
The mean consumption of sodium fluoride by F0
females during gestation was 3.4-28.0 mg/kg/day; mean
consumption by F1 females during gestation was
3.4-25.8 mg/kg/day (Table 1).

Overall mean feed consumption during gestation was
less in the F0 and F1 treated groups than in the control

group. The differences were not dose related and only
the feed consumption of F1 females in the 175 ppm
group was significantly decreased (Table 2). Initial body
weight (gestation day 0) and total body weight gain
(days 0-20) were similar in all F0 and F1 groups except
for the statistically significant decrease in body weight
gain of F1 females at 175 ppm (Table 2). The significant
decreases in mean feed consumption and body weight
gain of the F1 females of the 175 ppm group were con-
sidered random because of the lack of an effect in the
250 ppm group. Gravid uterine weight showed no dose-
related differences (Table 2).

### 3.3. Maternal and reproductive findings

Sodium fluoride at the doses given did not affect
reproduction of either F0 or F1 dams (Tables 3 and 4).
In F0 and F1 dams, the mean number of corpora lutea,
mean number of implants, implantation efficiency, mean
number of viable fetuses, and average percentage of
early and late deaths per litter showed no dose-related
differences. The number of F0 and F1 females with one
and with two early plus late deaths (combined) was
similar in all groups.

Sex distribution of F1 and F2 fetuses showed no dose-
related response. No F1 litters were totally resorbed.
Three F2 litters were totally resorbed, one litter each in
the 0, 25 and 175 ppm groups. Fetal body weight and
crown-rump length of F1 and F2 fetuses were not
affected (Tables 3 and 4). The single statistically sig-
nificant decrease in crown-rump length of F2 females at
175 ppm was considered random. Very few runts were
observed among the F1 or F2 litters, and the incidence
of runts was not related to dose.

non-ossified, bipartite, or malformed sternebrae was
similar in control and treated groups (Table 5). The
number of litters containing fetuses with malaligned
sternebrae was greater in all treated groups than in the
control group, but the increases were not dose related.
One fetus with fused sternebrae in the control group
was considered random and was eliminated from
Table 5.

When the incidences of sternebral variations in fetuses
and the number of fetuses with at least one, at least two,
and at least three variations per litter were analyzed, no
dose-related increases were seen (Table 6). The sig-
nificant increase in the percentage of litters with at least

one variation at 175 ppm was considered random due to
lack of dose relationship. These variations were based
on the classification of types (e.g. bipartite, malaligned,
etc., in Table 5) and not on individual sternebrae affec-
ted. As an additional measure of ossification deficiency,
data on the individual sternebrae that were bipartite or
showed reduced or lack of ossification were combined
and analyzed. No dose-related effects were seen (data
not shown).

No dose-related changes were noted in the develop-

## 4. Discussion

This study was done to determine any generational
effects of NaF on the development of the offspring. The
fluid route was selected to simulate human exposure.
The F2 litters represent the effects of in situ exposure
throughout gestation whose parents also had been
exposed in situ.

Reproductive parameters (corpora lutea, implants,

effect level (NOAEL) was greater than 300 ppm for rats.

response of the animals due to continuous exposure

through two generations; this was also shown in the
reproduction results of the study published elsewhere
(Collins et al., 2001). Morphological development and
development of the soft tissues was also not affected by
the continuous exposure of rats to NaF through several
generations.

Because of the chemical affinity of NaF for calcium
compounds, and the binding of fluoride with teeth and
bones within 24 h of ingestion (National Research
Council, 1993), effects are more likely to be observed in
these organs than in soft tissues. In the 1995 study, the
average number of fetuses per litter with three or more
skeletal variations was significantly increased in the 250
ppm group, and this effect contributed to the effect level
of the study. In the 2001 study, the average number of
fetuses per litter with three or more skeletal variations in
the 250 ppm group was double that of the control group
(1.0 vs 0.5 per litter) but this increase was insufficient to
make the value statistically significant.

No effect on the hyoid bone was observed in any sin-
gle-generation study (Collins et al., 1995; Heindel et al.,
1996), but decreased ossification of the hyoid bone was
seen in F2 fetuses at 175 and 250 ppm; that is, after
exposure for more than one generation. Only the
decrease at 250 ppm was statistically significant, so this
dose level was determined to be an effect level. The bio-
logical significance of the decreased ossification of this
bone is not known, but because it appeared after several
generations of animals had ingested high levels of NaF,
the effect could not be ignored.

In summary, sodium fluoride in drinking water at 175
ppm produced no compound-related effects when
administered to a parental and two filial generations,
and this dose is considered the no-effect level for devel-
opmental toxicity. Because of the decreased ossification
of the hyoid bone in the F2 fetuses, 250 ppm is
considered an effect level for developmental toxicity.
