## Abstract

Inorganic phosphate salts are widely used as food ingredients and in a variety of commercial applications. The United States
Food and Drug Administration (FDA) considers inorganic phosphates "Generally Recognized As Safe" (GRAS) (FDA, 1973a, 1979) [FDA: Food and Drug Administration 1973a. GRAS (Generally Recognized as Safe) food ingredients-phosphates. NTIS
PB-221-224, FDA, Food and Drug Administration, 1979. Phosphates; Proposed Affirmation of and Deletion From GRAS Status
as Direct and Human Food Ingredients. Federal Register 44 (244). 74845-74857, 18 December (1979)] and the European Union
(EU) allows inorganic phosphates to be added directly to food (EU Directive 95/2/EC as amended by 98/72/EC). In this review,
data on the acute, subchronic and chronic toxicity, genotoxicity, teratogenicity and reproductive toxicity from the published lit-
erature and from unpublished studies by the manufacturers are reviewed. Based on the toxicity data and similar chemistry, the
inorganic phosphates can be separated into four major classes, consisting of monovalent salts, divalent salts, ammonium salts and
aluminum salts. The proposed classification scheme supports the use of toxicity data from one compound to assess the toxicity of
another compound in the same class. However, in the case of eye and skin irritation, the proposed classification scheme cannot be
used because a wide range of responses exists within each class. Therefore, the eye and skin hazards associated with an individual
inorganic phosphate should be assessed on a chemical-by-chemical basis. A large amount of toxicity data exists for all four classes
of inorganic phosphates. The large and comprehensive database allows an accurate assessment of the toxicity of each class of
inorganic phosphate. Overall, all four classes of inorganic phosphates exhibit low oral, inhalation and dermal toxicities. Based on
these data, humans are unlikely to experience adverse effects when the daily phosphorus consumption remains below 70 mg/kg/day
(JECFA, 1964, 1982a) [JECFA (Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee on Food Additives 1964. Specifications for the Identity and
Purity of Food Additives and their Toxicological Evaluation) Emulsifiers, Stabilizers, Bleaching, and Maturing Agents. Technical
Report Series of the World Health Organization 281; ECFA (Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee on Food Additives 1982a.
Phosphoric Acid and Phosphate Salts. ICS/FA/82)]. C) 2001 Elsevier Science Ltd. All rights reserved.

### 1. Introduction

Phosphate is an essential nutrient to all living systems.
Since phosphate cannot be synthesized, the need for this
nutrient is met by ingestion of phosphate in the diet,
both naturally and as added phosphate. Inorganic
phosphate salts have been used as food ingredients for
decades and are considered "Generally Recognized As
Safe" (GRAS) by the US Food and Drug Administra-
tion (FDA) (FDA, 1973a, 1979).

Inorganic phosphate salts are widely used in a variety
of commercial applications including cleaning products,
toothpaste, fire extinguishers, textile processing, water
treatment and horticulture fertilizers.

In this review, the toxicology data supporting the
safety of inorganic phosphates will be considered. Data
on the acute, subchronic and chronic toxicity, geno-
toxicity, teratogenicity and reproductive toxicity from
the published literature and from unpublished studies
by the manufacturers will be critically reviewed. These
data demonstrate that there is a wealth of safety data on
the inorganic phosphates and that they are safe for their
intended uses.

### 2. Phosphate chemistry

The ortho phosphate anion is the basic anionic unit of
all phosphates. It is a tetrahedron structure of one
phosphorus atom surrounded by four oxygen atoms
(Fig. 1). The anion is tribasic and the three valences can
be satisfied by hydrogen, metal ions or a combination of
the two (Ellinger, 1972). The tetrahedron structure
allows the formation of polymers. Thus, salts of the
phosphate anion can involve one, two (also known as
pyrophosphates) or three (also known as tripolyphos-
phates) units of the phosphate anion in which each unit
is linked by shared oxygen atoms. The structures of the
monomer, dimer and trimer of phosphate anion are
shown in Fig. 1.

The term "condensed phosphate" is used to refer to
phosphates with two or more phosphate anion units.
Polyphosphates can be described by the generic formula:
M(n+2)PO(3n+1). These compounds exist as glassy, amor-
phous or fibrous materials. They are mixtures of a number
of polyphosphates of varying chain length. Because they
cannot crystallize, they are often called glassy or amor-
phous phosphates (Ellinger, 1972). Phosphate polymers

can also form cyclic polymers, such as sodium trimeta-
phosphate and sodium tetrametaphosphate.

The four major classes of cations forming salts with
the ortho and condensed phosphate anions are mono-
valent cations (sodium, potassium and hydrogen), diva-
lent cations (calcium and magnesium), ammonium and
aluminum. We grouped the inorganic phosphates into
four classes, based on the cation. Table 1 shows the salts
in each of these classes and the chemical formulas.
Throughout this review, the abbreviations for each che-
mical listed in Table 1 will be used when referring to each
chemical. The phosphoric acids have been grouped with
the other monovalent cations based on valence state.

The classification scheme was based on the similar
chemical and toxicological properties of inorganic
phosphates within a given class. In each class, ortho and
condensed salts are grouped together. This secondary
grouping is based on the chemicals' similar toxicological
properties and on the fact that condensed phosphates
break down to ortho phosphates in vivo (Ellinger,
1972). Thus, condensed and ortho salts can be viewed as
having commonality by the cation.

The toxicology data for each of the following endpoints

### 3. Acute toxicity of inorganic phosphates

The acute toxicity of the four classes of inorganic
phosphates (monovalent cation, divalent cation,
ammonium and aluminum) are presented in Tables 2- 18. For a given route of exposure, individual tables are
used to present the acute toxicity data for each of the
four classes of inorganic phosphates. A brief summary
of the data for a given route of exposure precedes each
set of tables.

The study designs of the different acute studies varied
and were based on the state-of-the-art at the time the
study was conducted. While it may appear that the
acute toxicity may vary across different studies for a
given compound, the differences are partly an artefact
of the doses chosen. For example, SAPP has oral LD50
values of >1000 mg/kg to >4000 mg/kg. These two
studies are not necessarily in conflict but merely reflect
the fact that less than half the animals died when dosed
at the highest dose tested in each individual study. Such
differences in study design influence the results.

The eye irritation studies reported in Tables 11-14
generally followed the standard eye irritation protocols
using the Draize test. Test material (0.1 g solid or 0.1 ml
liquid) was instilled into the conjunctival sac of one eye
of six rabbits. The other eye served as a control. The
eyelid was held shut for a few seconds and released.
Washing, when indicated, occurred within 20-30 s of
exposure and consisted of flooding the eye with large
amounts of water for 1 min. The summary tables indi-
cate if washing occurred and results for both washed
and unwashed eyes are included. Scores are based on
the degree or extent of opacity on the cornea, the red-
ness on the iris and the chemosis and discharge on the
conjunctiva, using a standard scale at intervals of 1, 24,
48 and 72 h or until all irritation resolves. More
emphasis is placed on the opacity of the cornea, which is
given a maximum of 80. Conjunctival changes can have
a maximum of 20 and iritis can have a maximum of 10.
The total maximum score is 110.

A broad range of eye irritation responses exists within
each class. For example, in the monovalent inorganic
phosphate class, PA is corrosive; whereas, MKP is
slightly to non-irritating. Eye irritation potential for the
inorganic phosphates should be evaluated on a chemi-
cal-by-chemical basis.

#### 3.5. Primary skin irritation

Primary skin irritation data exist in rabbits for each
of the four classes of inorganic phosphates (Tables 15-
18). In addition, primary skin irritation data are avail-
able for both ortho and condensed monovalent, divalent,
and ammonium and aluminum inorganic phosphates.

The skin irritation studies reported in Tables 15-18
generally followed standard skin irritation protocols.
Briefly, the backs of six rabbits were clipped free of hair.
Each test material was placed on two one-inch square
sites on the same animal at a dose of 0.5 g solid or 0.5 ml

liquid. One site was intact and the other site was abraded.
Each test site was covered with cotton gauze pads and
secured with tape in contact with the skin for 24 h. The
entire trunk of the animal was wrapped with rubberized
cloth or other occlusive impervious material to retard
evaporation of the test substance. After the 24-h expo-
sure period, the wrappings were removed and the sites
were scored after approximately 1 h for erythema and
edema, using a standard Draize scoring system on a
scale of 0-4 for each parameter (maximum of 8.0 Pri-
mary Irritation Index). Scoring was repeated at 24, 48
and 72 h post-exposure or until all irritation resolved.

For some salts, many studies were conducted.
Because the data have come from many companies,
using different protocols (e.g. time of exposure, dose,
intact vs abraded skin, occlusive vs non-occlusive
dressing), results sometimes vary. A broad range of
skin irritation responses exists within each class. For
example, in the monovalent inorganic phosphate
class PPA is corrosive, whereas MKP is slightly to non-
irritating. Skin irritation potential for the inorganic
phosphates should be evaluated on a chemical-by-
chemical basis.

#### 3.6. Skin sensitization

PA is one of the two inorganic phosphates that has
been tested for skin sensitization. PA did not cause skin
sensitization when applied to human skin (IUCLID,
1995a).

APP was tested and did not cause skin sensitization in
the guinea pig (Albright & Wilson, 1993a,e). An aque-
ous APP solution (with urea) was negative when applied
in a 46-h exposure human skin patch test (Albright &
Wilson, 1996).

#### 5.2. Aluminum inorganic phosphates

##### 5.2.1. Ortho

Male Sprague-Dawley rats (25/group) were adminis-
tered 14,470 ppm aluminum hydroxide (positive con-
trol), or 0.7 or 3.0% SALP in the diet for 28 days
(Stauffer Chemical Company, 1986). Control animals

3.0% (1543 mg/kg/day) SALP in the diet.

oral toxicity study was conducted with groups of albino
rats fed SALP at dietary levels of 0.3, 1.0 and 3.0% in
the diet (Solutia Inc., 1972b). Microscopic examination
of tissues and organs disclosed microconcretions in the
renal tubules of the female rats from all three test groups.

incidence and severity were dose related. No other
abnormalities were found (Solutia Inc., 1972b). In a
follow-up study, female rats were fed diets containing
0.03 and 0.1% of SALP. No effects were observed in
survival, growth rate or kidney lesions of the treated
animals (Solutia Inc., 1973f).

90-day oral administration of SALP to pure-bred
beagle dogs at dietary levels of 0.3, 1.0 and 3.0%

revealed no significant abnormalities (Solutia Inc.,
1972c).

#### 5.3. Summary of subchronic toxicity of inorganic phosphates

Results of multiple studies in rats, dogs and sheep
ranging from 28 to 100 days demonstrated that the
kidney is a target organ at high doses. At high phos-
phate loads, excess phosphate causes increased bone
demineralization and release of calcium as part of a
physiological regulatory mechanism. Excessive phos-
phate and calcium loads result in nephrocalcinosis and
other renal effects.

All of the phosphates, with the exception of SALP,
exhibited similar NOEL/NOAELs. SALP had a NOEL
that was significantly higher than the NOEL/NOAELs
for the other inorganic phosphates, presumably due to
the high contribution of aluminum ion to the salt's
molecular weight and the poor absorption of aluminum
ion. For the majority of the tested inorganic phos-
phates, the NOEL/NOAELs were based on renal
effects. As the renal effects are due to excess phosphate
and calcium load and not a direct effect of the cation, it
is expected that all four classes of inorganic phosphates
would produce the same critical effect at high doses.
Therefore, a single NOEL/NOAEL can be established
for all four classes of inorganic phosphates based on the
available data. Based on the lowest subchronic NOEL/
NOAELs observed for three inorganic phosphates (i.e.
STP, STMP and SHMP), the group subchronic NOEL/
NOAEL is established at greater or equal to 103 mg/kg/
day.

It is important to note that wide dose spacing (i.e. a
log-fold difference between the NOEL/NOAEL and
LOEL) was used in the studies driving the NOEL/
NOAEL. The wide dose spacing was used because these
subchronic studies were simply preliminary studies used
to established an appropriate dose range for the chronic
studies (Hodge, 1964).

Therefore, the subchronic NOEL/NOAELs for STP,
STMP and SHMP are most likely considerably higher
than estimated from the preliminary studies. This is
further supported by the NOEL/NOAELs established
in the chronic studies for STP, STMP and SHMP,
which are several fold greater than the subchronic
NOEL/NOAELs (see next section).

6.1. Monovalent inorganic phosphates: ortho and
condensed

Rats were administered PA in the diet for more than
12 months (Ellinger, 1972). The concentration of phos-
phate-based compounds in the basal diet was not indi-
cated. No adverse effects were noted at up to 0.75%.
The NOEL for this study was 0.75% PA or more in the
diet, which was equivalent to 386 mg/kg/day or more,
assuming a 0.35-kg rat consumes 18 g food/day.

Ten female rabbits were administered 300-700 mg/kg
MSP in increasing doses via drinking water for 5-16
months (Fazekas, 1954). The only parameter assessed in
the study was the weight of the parathyroid glands. The
average weight was 295% of control values. The tox-
icological significance of an increase in parathyroid
gland weight is related to the role of this gland in the
regulation of the calcium/phosphorus balance. The
excess addition of phosphorus to the diet is expected to
stimulate the parathyroid gland to increase hormone
output, which promotes renal excretion of phosphate.
Therefore, the increase in parathyroid gland weight is a
physiological response to the added phosphate. It is
difficult to determine a NOAEL for this study. This is
considered a poor study since only one endpoint was
evaluated.

Rats were administered 8% sodium orthophosphate
or sodium metaphosphate in the diet for 7 months (see
FDA, 1975a). The treatment caused bone decalcifica-
tion with metastatic calcium deposits, hypertrophy and
hyperplasia of the parathyroid glands, and renal cal-
cium deposition. The LOEL for this study was 8%,
which was equivalent to 4114 mg/kg/day, assuming a
0.35-kg rat consumes 18 g food/day.

Fifteen beagle dogs (sex not specified) were adminis-
tered diets providing 800 mg/kg/day of DKP for 14 or
38 weeks (Schneider et al., 1980a,b). An unspecified
number of control dogs were included in the study. The
dogs vomited, were cachectic, and had elevated creati-
nine and blood urea nitrogen. Necropsies were per-
formed at the end of the experiments. Kidneys were
processed for histological evaluation (i.e. various stains,
reactions and electron microscopy). Renal damage con-
sisted of disseminated tubular atrophy (usually of the
proximal tubules), focal scar tissue, and nephrocalci-
nosis. Renal morphological changes were seen in the
treated dogs after 14 and 38 weeks; however, renal
damage was greater after 38 weeks. The effect level for
this study was 800 mg/kg/day.

Male rats (12/group) were administered diets con-
taining 0.87% (basal diet) or 5.1% DKP for 150 days
(Dymsza et al., 1959). The basal diet contained 0.42%
of phosphorus (due to the 0.87% of DKP) and 0.56%
of calcium. Kidney weight was significantly increased in
the 5.1% treatment group. However, no histopatholo-
gical lesions were noted in the kidney. No other treat-

ment-related effects were noted during the gross or
histopathological examinations. The NOAEL for this
study was 5.1% in the diet, which was equivalent to
2623 mg/kg/day, assuming a 0.35-kg rat consumes 18 g
food/day.

Rats (50/sex/group) were administered 0, 0.05, 0.5 or
5.0% STP in the diet for 2 years (Hodge, 1964). Treat-
ment-related effects were only noted in animals of the
5% group. Those animals exhibited decreased growth,
increased food consumption, signs of anemia, increased
kidney weights, reduced bone growth and renal lesions.
Histopathological changes in the kidney consisted of
dilated convoluted tubules, hyaline casts, interstitial
fibrosis between the dilated tubules, fibrotic glomeruli
and intertubular calcification. Tumor incidence and
type were similar in controls and treated animals. The
NOEL for this study was 0.5% in the diet, which was
equivalent to 257 mg/kg/day, assuming a 0.35-kg rat
consumes 18 g food/day.

Rats (50/sex/group) were administered 0, 0.1, 1.0 or
10.0% STMP in the diet for 2 years (Hodge, 1964).
Males fed 1.0% exhibited slight growth retardation
during the first year, with growth returning to normal
during the second year. Males and females fed 10.0%
STMP exhibited substantial growth retardation.
Decreased liver weights in males and females and
retarded bone growth in females of the 10% groups
were noted. Hematological and urine parameters were
normal in all animals. No treatment-related histopatho-
logical changes were noted in any group. Tumor
incidence and type were similar in controls and treated
animals. No effects were noted in the 0.1% group. As
the slight growth retardation in the 1.0% group was not
accompanied by any other effects, the NOAEL for this
study was 1.0% in the diet or 514 mg/kg/day, assuming
a 0.35-kg rat consumes 18 g food/day.

Rats (50/sex/group) were administered 0, 0.05, 0.5 or
5.0% SHMP in the diet for 2 years (Hodge, 1964).
Decreased growth and increased food consumption
were noted in animals fed 5% SHMP. Increased kidney
to body weight ratios were noted in the 0.5 and 5%
groups; however, the authors stated that the organ
weight changes were difficult to interpret due to a varied
incidence of kidney infection. Calcification of the renal
tubules was noted in most rats in the 5% group and one
rat in the 0.5% group. Hematological and urine para-
meters and bone growth were normal in all animals.
Tumor incidence and type were similar in controls and
treated animals. No effects were noted in the 0.05%
group. As the increased kidney to body weight ratio in
the 0.5% group was not accompanied by a significant
observation of histopathological renal damage (i.e. only
one animal exhibited renal calcification), the NOAEL
for this study was 0.5% in the diet. This is equivalent to
257 mg/kg/day, assuming a 0.35-kg rat consumes 18 g
food/day.

Male rats (12/group) were administered 0, 0.93 or
3.5% SHMP in the diet for 150 days (Dymsza et al.,
1959). The concentrations of phosphate and calcium in
the basal diet were 0.42 and 0.56%, respectively. Kidney
weight was significantly increased in the 3.5% treatment
group. No histopathological lesions were noted in the
kidney. No other treatment-related effects were noted
during the gross or histopathological examinations. The
NOAEL for this study was 3.5% in the diet, which was
equivalent to 1800 mg/kg/day, assuming a 0.35-kg rat
consumes 18 g food/day.

Rats (34-36/sex/group) were administered 0, 3 or 5%
DSP, STP or TSPP in the diet for 6 months (Hahn,
1961). The concentration of phosphate-based com-
pounds in the basal diet was 0.49-0.63%. Groups
receiving 3 or 5% of the phosphates exhibited decreased
growth and renal calcification.

In an additional experiment, male rats (36/group)
were administered 0 or 1.8% DSP or TSPP in the diet
for 6 months (Hahn, 1961). A slight, yet statistically
significant, increase in kidney weight was noted at
1.8%, but no growth inhibition was seen. In a third
experiment, male rats (group size not specified) were
administered 1.1% DSP, STP or TSPP in the diet
for 39 weeks (Hahn, 1961). All three phosphates
caused a slight growth retardation during the first
part of the experiment and slight renal calcification after
39 weeks. The LOEL for these studies was 1.1% DSP,
STP or TSPP in the diet, which was equivalent to
566 mg/kg/day, assuming a 0.35-kg rat consumes 18 g
food/day.

### 6.2. Ammonium inorganic phosphates

Ten female rabbits were administered 300-700 mg/kg
DAP in increasing doses via drinking water for 5-16
months (Fazekas, 1954). The only parameter assessed in
the study was the weight of the parathyroid glands. The
average weight was 235% of control values. The
increase in parathyroid gland weight is a physiological
response to the added phosphate. This is considered a
poor study since only one endpoint was evaluated.
Therefore, it is not possible to determine effect and no-
effect levels.

### 6.3. Aluminum inorganic phosphates

#### 6.3.1. Ortho

Beagle dogs (4/sex/group) were administered 0, 0.3,
1.0 or 3.0% SALP in the diet for 26 weeks (Stauffer
Chemical Company, 1987). Minor decreased body
weights (81% of controls) and food consumption were
noted in males of the 3.0% group. Mild histopatho-
logical changes were noted only in males of the
3.0% group. Changes included hepatocyte vacuolation,
hepatocyte hypertrophy, bile stasis, seminiferous tubule

germinal epithelial cell degeneration and atrophy and
mild glomerulonephritis.

Most of the changes appeared to be an indirect result
of SALP treatment resulting from the decreased food
consumption and weight gain. Hematological, blood
chemistry, urinalysis and necropsy analysis did not
reveal any treatment-related effects in any group. Brain
and bone aluminum levels were similar between treated
and control male animals. Brain aluminum levels were
slightly greater (1.6-fold over control) in females of the
3.0% group. The NOEL for this study was 1.0% (mean
daily doses of 390 and 323 mg/kg/day for males and
females, respectively) in the diet.

Beagle dogs (6/sex/group) were administered 0, 0.3,
1.0 or 3.0% SALP in the diet for 27 weeks (Stauffer
Chemical Company, 1981). The concentration of phos-
phate- and aluminum-containing compounds in the
basal diet was not indicated. The only treatment-related
effects were sporadic decreases in mean daily food con-
sumption values of all female groups administered
SALP. Clinical observations, weight gains, food con-
sumption, hematology and clinical chemistry para-
meters, urinalysis, gross necropsy, fecal occult blood,
ophthalmic and histopathological findings were com-
parable between treated and control animals. The
NOEL for this study was 3% (mean daily doses of 1034
and 1087 mg/kg/day for males and females, respec-
tively) SALP in the diet.

### 6.4. Summary of chronic toxicity of inorganic phosphates

Results of multiple studies in rats, dogs and rabbits
ranging from 21 to 104 weeks demonstrated that the
kidney is a target organ at high doses. At high phos-
phate loads, excess phosphate causes increased bone
demineralization and release of calcium as part of a
physiological regulatory mechanism. Excessive phos-
phate and calcium loads result in nephrocalcinosis and
other renal effects.

A wide range of chronic NOEL/NOAELs was estab-
lished for the inorganic phosphates. The difference
between the lowest NOEL/NOAEL and the highest
NOEL/NOAEL is over an order of magnitude. Despite
this range, the majority of the NOEL/NOAELs were
based on the same endpoint (i.e. renal effects). Because
the renal effects are due to excess phosphate and cal-
cium load and not a direct effect of the cation, it is
expected that all four classes of inorganic phosphates
would produce the same critical effect at high doses.
Therefore, a single NOEL/NOAEL can be established
for all four classes of inorganic phosphates based on the
available data. Based on the lowest chronic NOEL/
NOAELs observed for two inorganic phosphates (i.e.
STP and SHMP), the group chronic NOEL/NOAEL is
established at 225 mg/kg/day.

## 7. Teratogenicity of inorganic phosphates

Teratogenicity data exist for the monovalent and
divalent cation inorganic phosphates. Teratogenicity
was primarily assessed in rats and mice; however, rab-
bits and hamsters were also used. None of the tested
inorganic phosphates produced teratogenic effects.

As all of the studies were conducted according to a
similar protocol, the general protocol, with species-
specific details, is outlined below. This is followed by a
table (Table 24), which summarizes the results of each
study.

### 7.1. Protocol outline

Animals were administered the test material, dis-
solved in an appropriate vehicle, by gavage. The test

material was administered on the following days of
gestation: mice - days 6 through 15, rat - days 6
through 15, hamster - days 6 through 10, rabbit -
days 6 through 18. Body weights and daily observations
of food consumption, appearance and behavior were
made. Caesarean section was performed on the follow-
ing days of gestation: mice - day 17, rats - day 20,
hamster - day 14, rabbit - day 29. After caesarean
section, the sex, body weights of live pups, and numbers
of corpora lutea, implantation sites, resorption sites and
live and dead fetuses were determined. All fetuses
underwent a detailed gross examination for external
congenital abnormalities. Visceral examinations using
the Wilson technique were performed on one-third of
the fetuses of each litter. The remaining fetuses were
processed for staining with Alizarin Red S dye to
determine skeletal defects.

### 7.3. Summary of teratogenicity of inorganic phosphates

Results of multiple studies in rats, mice, rabbits and
hamsters demonstrated that none of the tested inorganic
phosphates exhibit teratogenic potential when ade-
quately tested at significant dose levels. Based on these
results, it is not expected that other inorganic phos-
phates would be teratogenic. Therefore, all four classes
of inorganic phosphates are grouped together in regards
to teratogenicity.

## 8. Reproductive toxicity of inorganic phosphates

Reproductive toxicity data exist for the monovalent
cation inorganic phosphates. None of the tested inor-
ganic phosphates produced reproductive effects when
administered to rats.

### 8.1. Monovalent inorganic phosphates

#### 8.1.1. Ortho

An unspecified number of male and female rats were
administered 0.4 or 0.75% PA in the diet for 29 weeks
(Bonting and Jansen, 1956). Basal diets contained 1.9%
TCP and 0.8% DSP. After 29 weeks of treatment, the
rats were mated. Eleven weeks after the first mating, the
rats were mated again. It was not indicated whether the
parents received treated diet during the 11 weeks
between the first and second mating. The offspring were
maintained on the parental diet for 29 weeks starting at
3 weeks of age. All reproductive parameters (weight of
the mothers, number of living young and stillborn per
litter, average birth weight of the living young, and the
number of young left at weaning) were comparable
between controls and treated animals for both generations.

There were no remarkable body weight or gross
or histopathological differences between controls and
treated animals. Blood parameters, which were only
determined for controls and the 0.4% group, were
similar between the two groups. The NOEL for this
study was 0.75% PA in the diet (Bonting and Jansen,
1956).

#### 8.1.2. Condensed

Rats (16 females and eight males/group) were admi-
nistered STP, STMP or SHMP in the diet from weaning
to 100 days of age for three generations (Hodge, 1964).
STP and SHMP were administered at 0.5% and STMP

was administered at 0.05%. Matings were carried out
when the animals were 100 days of age. The third gen-
eration animals were sacrificed at 1 month of age. Two
litters from each generation were examined. None of the
inorganic phosphates affected fertility, litter size, or
growth or survival of the offspring.

For the third generation, organ weights and gross and
histopathological findings were comparable between
controls and treated animals. The NOELs for this study
were 0.5% for STP and SHMP and 0.05% for STMP.

### 8.2. Summary of reproductive toxicity of inorganic phosphates

Results of several studies in rats demonstrated that
none of the tested inorganic phosphates are reproduc-
tive toxicants. Based on these data, it is not expected
that other inorganic phosphates would be reproductive
toxicants. Therefore, it is suggested that all four classes
of inorganic phosphates are grouped together in regards
to reproductive toxicity.

## 9. Safety assessment

### 9.1. Quality of toxicological database on inorganic phosphates

Numerous toxicological studies exist on the toxicity
of inorganic phosphates. These data will be used in the
following sections to show that the four classes of inor-
ganic phosphates are safe for both food and commercial
applications.

The similar toxicity and chemistry of inorganic phos-
phates within a particular class allows data from one
compound to be used to assess the toxicity of another
compound in the same class. This approach is sup-
ported by the robust toxicological database, which shows
clearly that inorganic phosphates within a particular
class exhibit similar toxicities with similar magnitudes.

Even between classes, the inorganic phosphates exhibit
similar toxicological properties. All of the tested inor-
ganic phosphates are practically non-toxic following
acute oral, dermal and inhalation exposures. In sub-
chronic and chronic studies, high doses of the inorganic
phosphates did not cause severe toxicological effects. In
those studies, the common effect seen between classes
of inorganic phosphates was renal effects, particularly
calcification of the kidney, at high doses.

This effect is not a direct toxic effect of the inorganic
phosphates, but rather an indirect effect of increased
calcium excretion at high doses of inorganic phosphates
as part of a physiological regulatory mechanism. All of
the inorganic phosphates were negative in a variety of
genotoxicity assays and reproductive toxicity and ter-
atogenicity studies.

The two types of effects that are difficult to assess
between the classes of inorganic phosphates and even
within a particular class are ocular and dermal irrita-
tion. Because every class of inorganic phosphate, and
almost every individual phosphate assessed in this arti-
cle, has this type of data, this does not present a pro-
blem in assessing the toxicity of the inorganic
phosphates.

In conclusion, the toxicological database on the inor-
ganic phosphates is very robust. Based on the similar
chemistry and similar toxicological results within and
between classes of inorganic phosphates, the toxicities
of similar inorganic phosphates are readily predicted
from the available toxicological data. The only excep-
tion to this is for eye and skin irritation, which should
be assessed for each individual inorganic phosphate.

## 10. Current regulatory approvals

### 10.1. United States Food and Drug Administration

In 1975, the US FDA evaluated the safety of inor-
ganic phosphates for use as food ingredients (FDA,
1975a). Based on a thorough review of the scientific lit-
erature up to that time, the FDA concluded that many
different inorganic phosphates are considered GRAS.
Table 25 summarizes the Generally Recognized As Safe
(GRAS) status of the inorganic phosphates.

The Select Committee, a group of qualified scientists
that evaluated the GRAS status of chemicals for the
FDA, stated: "None of the GRAS phosphates is intrin-
sically harmful and their use in foods does not present a
hazard when the total amount of phosphorous ingested
and the intakes of calcium, magnesium, vitamin D, and
other nutrients are satisfactory." (FDA, 1975a).

In the FDA's opinion, none of the inorganic phos-
phates listed in Table 25 represent a health hazard when
added directly to food.

### 10.2. JECFA

The Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee on Food
Additives (JECFA) assessed the safety of using inor-
ganic phosphates in food several times (JECFA 1964,
1982a,b). The most recent review was completed in 1982
(JECFA, 1982a). JECFA estimated an Acceptable Daily
Intake (ADI) of phosphorous of 70 mg/kg that would
be unlikely to cause adverse effects in humans when
ingested daily over a lifetime (JECFA, 1964, 1982a).
This level was estimated by assuming that an adult
consumes 2800 calories per day and that humans can
tolerate up to 1% phosphorus in the diet (based on
animal data). JECFA's main concerns with the con-
sumption of inorganic phosphates focused on nephro-
calcinosis and bone loss. JECFA used the lowest feeding

level (1% P in the diet) that caused nephrocalcinosis as
the basis for their evaluation. However, they noted that
this was probably a conservative approach since the rat
is exquisitely susceptible to calcification and hydrone-
phrosis upon exposure to acids forming calcium chelates
or complexes. Regarding the bone loss, JECFA stated,
"In a review of the available literature no conclusions
were reached on the optimal Ca:P ratio or whether this
ratio is of any dietary significance in man."

Based on this information, JECFA derived an
Acceptable Total Dietary Phosphorous Load for man
of 0-70 mg/kg.

under Directive 95/2/EC as amended by Directive 98/

The lower subchronic group NOEL/NOAEL of 103

these studies were simply preliminary studies used to
established a dose range for chronic studies with these
compounds (Hodge, 1964).

Assuming an average body weight of 0.4 kg and a
daily food consumption of 18 g/day, a rat consuming a
diet containing 0.5% of STP or SHMP would ingest 225
mg STP or SHMP/kg/day. This would be equivalent to
57 mg P/kg/day from STP or 68 mg P/kg/day from
SHMP after accounting for the percentage of P in each
compound. These daily animal phosphorus intakes can
be extrapolated to give a human ADI by dividing by
two uncertainty factors (UFs) as shown in the following
equation and described below:

## Human ADI (mg P/kg/day)

Animal NOAEL (mg P/kg/day)

¼
(UFInterspecies) x (UF Intraspecies)

A UF of 1 is used for interspecies differences. As rats
are more susceptible to renal calcification caused by
inorganic phosphates (JECFA, 1982a,b), a UF of less
than 1 could justifiably be used to extrapolate the ani-
mal data to humans. However, the magnitude of this
UF cannot be determined based on the available data;
therefore, as a conservative approach, a UF of 1 is used
for the extrapolation.

A UF of 1 is used for intraspecies differences. As all
mammals require inorganic phosphorus for basic
physiological functions, it is not expected that large dif-
ferences in sensitivities will exist within a population.

The composite UF is 1x1=1. Therefore, the human
ADI suggested here is equivalent to the animal NOEL/
NOAEL. Averaging the SHMP and STP animal
NOEL/NOAELs, the human ADI is equivalent to 63
mg P/kg/day. This value is similar to the JECFA ADI
of 0-70 mg P/kg/day.

The FDA reported that the average per capita daily
intake of phosphorus due to inorganic phosphates
added to food is approximately 300 mg (4.3 mg P/kg
assuming a body weight of 70 kg) (FDA, 1975a). A
daily per capita intake of phosphorus added to food of
300 mg, equivalent to 4.3 mg P/kg/day, is approxi-
mately 16 times less than the JEFCA ADI of 70 mg P/
kg/day. Therefore, a person would have to greatly

### 12. Safety of inorganic phosphates for consumer product uses

Inorganic phosphates are used in many different con-
sumer products. The review of the oral, dermal and
inhalation toxicity of the inorganic phosphates clearly
shows that these compounds exhibit very low toxicity by
all three routes of exposure. Systemic exposure to inor-
ganic phosphates due to consumer product use is likely
to be much lower than the systemic exposure due to
food additive use.

Therefore, based on the low exposure and low sys-
temic toxicity, very little concern is associated with
systemic toxicity after exposure to inorganic phos-
phates at levels expected to be present in consumer
products.

The main concern associated with consumer products
containing inorganic phosphates is eye and skin effects.
The review of the eye and skin irritation data of the
inorganic phosphates indicates that the inorganic
phosphate class cannot be used to predict the eye and
skin irritation potential of a given inorganic phosphate.
Therefore, it is important to determine the eye and
skin irritation properties of each inorganic phosphate
that is used in a consumer product. With that data,
the potential hazards of the consumer product con-
taining the inorganic phosphate can be accurately
determined.

## 13. Conclusions

A large number of toxicological studies exist for all
four classes of inorganic phosphates. The toxicity data

clearly show that inorganic phosphates within a given
class exhibit similar toxicities. This similarity allows
existing data to be used to predict the toxicity of other
compounds in the same class and also across classes.
For acute (oral, dermal and inhalation), subchronic
toxicity, chronic toxicity, teratogenicity, reproductive
toxicity and genotoxicity all four classes of inorganic
phosphates show similar results in standard tests. The
basic phosphate unit common to all of the four classes
of inorganic phosphates allows one to use a common
ADI for all compounds. Based on our review, the only
exception to this similarity of toxicity is the eye and skin
irritation potential, which cannot be readily predicted
based on the class of a particular compound.

Overall, all four classes of inorganic phosphates exhi-
bit low oral, inhalation and dermal toxicities. In addi-
tion, the long history of safe use of inorganic
phosphates in food lends further support to the safety of
inorganic phosphates. The direct addition of inorganic
phosphates to food at current levels giving rise to
intakes well below the ADI of 70 mg/kg/day is unlikely
to cause adverse effects in humans.

The hazards associated with inorganic phosphates in
consumer products should be determined on a case-by-
case basis. Minimal concern is associated with systemic
toxicity after exposure to consumer products. The main
concern is with eye and skin effects. Toxicity data on
individual inorganic phosphates should be used to
assess the hazards associated with specific products.
