# Original Article


# Antifungal Triazole Derivative Triadimefon Induces Ectopic Maxillary Cartilage by Altering the Morphogenesis of the First Branchial Arch

Francesca Di Renzo, Maria L. Broccia, Erminio Giavini, and Elena Menegola*
University of Milan, Department of Biology, Italy

BACKGROUND: The triazole derivative, triadimefon (FON), induces branchial arch abnormalities in post-implantation
rat embryos cultured in vitro, and cranio-facial malformations in mouse fetuses. Ectopic maxillary cartilage has been also
described as a typical FON-related malformation. This work studies the morphogenesis of the ectopic cartilage in rat
embryos and fetuses exposed in vivo to FON during the early postimplantation period. METHODS: Pregnant rats were
treated with 0, 250, and 500 mg/kg FON on Day 9.5 of pregnancy (D9.5) and sacrificed at term (D20), during the early
fetal period (D17) or at different embryogenetic periods (D10, D11, D12). The skeleton was examined after stain of bone
and cartilage or of cartilage alone respectively at term or at D17. The neural crest cell (NCC) migration and compaction
was investigated at D10 and D11 and the cranial nerve organization described at D12. RESULTS: Triadimefon is
teratogenic in rats under the chosen experimental conditions. The malformations were at the level of the cranio-facial and
axial skeleton at term and of the hindbrain nerves in embryos. A NCC abnormal migration and compaction was
observed at the level of the first branchial arch: in FON-exposed embryos NCC were detected at the level of both
maxillary and mandibular processes, whereas control embryos showed the immunostained tissue only at the level of the
mandibular bud. CONCLUSIONS: The pathogenic pathway proposed to explain the ectopic cartilage is the
displacement of part of the NCC-derived tissues at the maxillary region of the first branchial arch. Birth Defects Res (Part B)
80:2-11, 2007.

## INTRODUCTION

Antifungal triazole-derivatives are teratogenic agents,
believed to act by inhibiting the embryonic catabolismof
the morphogen retinoic acid (RA) (for an extensive
review see Menegola et al., 2006).

The metabolite of vitamin A, RA acts as a morphogen
in vertebrates (Ross et al., 2000). A direct relationship
between RA concentration, Hox gene expression, cranio-
facial (Osumi-Yamashita, 1996), vertebral (Kessel and
Gruss, 1991; Kawanishi et al., 2003) or limb (Niederreither
et al., 2002) specification has been documented.

Craniofacial bones in vertebrates with jaws (gnatho-
stomes) originate entirely from the frontal and branchial
arch ectomesenchyme, a mesenchymal tissue formed
by the condensation of cells (neural crest cells [NCC],
a migratory and multipotent cell population unique
to vertebrates) migrated from the brain (Hall, 1999;
Le Douarin and Kalcheim, 1999; Le Douarin and Dupin,
2003; Santagati and Rijli, 2003) (Fig. 1). Specifically, in all
vertebrates cranial NCC migrate in a conserved pattern of
three streams: the mandibular (or trigeminal) stream,

emerging from the mesencephalon and from the hind-
brain rhombomere (r) 2, migrates to the anterior head and
to the first branchial arch; the hyoid stream, directed from
r4 to the second branchial arch; and the common branchial
stream, directed from r6 to the branchial arches III-VI
(Lumsden et al., 1991; Epperlein et al., 2000; Meulemans
and Bronner-Fraser, 2002; Trainor et al., 2002) (Fig. 1).

Neural crest cells that migrate to the first branchial
arch form a dorsal (maxillary) and a ventral (mandibular)
ectomesenchymal condensation (Francis-West et al.,
1998; Chai et al., 2000; Meulemans and Bronner-Fraser,
2002). Recently, the assumption that the embryonic
cartilaginous skeleton of the upper jaw (palatoquadrate
cartilage) is formed from the maxillary component
and that the embryonic lower jaw skeleton (Meckel's

RA exposure both in vivo and in vitro. In particular, the
in vitro exposure of postimplantation rat embryos to
a number of triazole derivatives induces specific
malformations at the branchial apparatus (fusion bet-
ween the first and second branchial arch, hypoplasic
branchial arch I or II), at the associated cranial nerves
(V, VII, IX, X, fused, and disorganized), related to the
incorrect migration of NCC streams from hindbrain to
the branchial arches and to the abnormal segmentation
of the rhombencephalic vesicle (Tiboni, 1993; Menegola

mouse embryos with two triazole antifungals (triadime-
fon [FON] and fluconazole) during the early embryo-
genetic period, induced craniofacial (Menegola et al.,
2005b; Tiboni and Giampietro, 2005) and axial abnor-
malities (Menegola et al., 2005b). Interestingly, maxillary
ectopic cartilage, similar to that observed by Morriss-Kay
(1993) after RA exposure, was detected after in vivo

exposure of mouse embryos to FON (Menegola et al.,
2005a).

The present work studies the genesis of ectopic
cartilage in rat embryos and fetuses exposed in vivo
during the early embryogenetic period to the triazole
derivative FON. The rat was chosen because of the more
synchronous development of embryos in this species in
comparison to mouse.


## MATERIALS AND METHODS

CD:Crl rats (Charles River, Calco, Italy), housed in a
thermostatically maintained room (T =22+2℃, relative
humidity = 55+5%) with a 12-hr light cycle (light from
6:00 a.m. to 6:00 p.m.), with food (Italiana Mangimi,
Settimo Milanese, Italy) and tap water ad libitum, were
used for this study. Virgin females were mated overnight
with males of proven fertility. The day of positive vaginal
smear was considered as Day 0 of gestation (D0).

Pregnant females were treated at D9.5 (5:00 p.m.) with 0,
250, and 500 mg/kg body weight FON (purity = 96.1%;
gift of Bayer, Italy), dissolved in acetone diluted 1:9 in
corn oil. The final volume of treatment was 5 ml/kg body
weight. The controls were treated with the same volume
of vehicle. Females were checked for signs of toxicity
after the treatment until the sacrifice and were weighted
at the start of gestation, at treatment time and at times of
sacrifice. Some females from control and FON 250 and
500 groups were sacrificed during the embryogenetic
period, at D10 (1:00 p.m.), D11 (10:00 a.m.), and D12
(10:00 a.m.). After explantation, embryos were morpho-
logically examined and, as an indicator of developmental
degree, the somite number was recorded. D10 and
D11 embryos were processed for the whole mount
NCC staining by using antibodies anti-CRABP (cellular
retinoic acid binding protein) marker for NCC as
described by Wei et al. (1999) and Menegola et al.
(2003). D12 embryos were used for the whole mount
immunostaining of cranial nerves and ganglia, according
to the method proposed by Mark et al. (1993). Immu-
nostaining procedures are summarized in Table 1.

Some females of control and FON 500 groups were
sacrificed during the fetal period (D17) for the staining
of cartilage. Females from all groups were sacrificed
at term of gestation (D20), when the litter weight, number
of implantations, resorptions, live, and dead fetuses
were recorded. The postimplantation loss index [(total
implantation-live fetuses/total implantation) × 100] was
calculated. At D20, after external examination, live fetuses
were weighed and all processed for skeletal double
staining of bone and cartilage. The nomenclature used
for identify skeletal elements was in accordance to
Depew et al. (2002).

Quantitative parameters, expressed as media±
standard deviation (SD), were analyzed by using the
statistical test ANOVA followed by Tukey's test. Quali-
tative parameters were expressed as percentage and
statistically analyzed by using x2 test. The level of
significance was set at P<0.05.


## Staining of Cartilaginous Elements in D17 Fetuses

After sacrifice, fetuses were dehydrated overnight in
ethanol 96% and maintained in the staining solution at
pH 2.8 (Alcian blue GX [0.14% dissolved in ethanol 70%]
[Sigma, St. Louis, MO]:acetic acid:ethanol 70% [5:8:50])
for a few days until staining, in 0.2% KOH until
maceration of soft tissues, and then in clearing solution
(ethanol 70%:glycerin:benzyl alcohol [2:2:1]). Samples
were conserved in ethanol 70%:glycerin (1:1).


## Double Staining of Bone and Cartilage in D20 Fetuses

The double staining of bone and cartilage was carried
out according to the method of Kimmel and Trammel
(1981), partially modified as described previously
(Menegola et al., 2001b).


## RESULTS Results at Term of Gestation (D20)

To verify the teratogenic potential of FON on rat
skeletons, 15 females for each group were treated on

D9.5 and sacrificed at term of gestation (D20). No signs
of maternal toxicity was detected in the treated group,
as shown by clinical signs and by the weight gain after
treatment (data not shown). Implantation number,
postimplantation loss index, and fetal and placental
weight were similar to control for both treated groups
(Table 2). Fetuses explanted from dams dosed with FON
showed, after external examination, abnormalities at the
level of the head. The malformations, dose-related,
included microstomia and cleft palate (Table 3). Cranio-
facial-specific and severe abnormalities were observed
after skeleton staining in most of the fetuses explanted
from FON-treated mothers (Table 4, Fig. 2D-I). The
abnormal structures were the upper and lower jaws,
the tympanic ring, and the base of the skull. The
abnormalities were abnormal shape, reduction, agenesis,
and fusion of skeletal elements. Ectopic cartilage at
the level of upper jaw was observed in both treated
groups, visible as ectopic cartilaginous fragments or as a
large element. Fetuses (1% and 38%, respectively) of
group FON 250 and FON 500 was characterized by a
large shape cartilage joining the upper and lower jaw
together (Table 4, Fig. 2D,E,G,H). The presence of
ectopic cartilage was always coupled with ectopic
cartilaginous elements at the base of the skull. These
elements were usually fused with the ectopic cartilage
(Fig. 2F,I).

As far as the axial elements are concerned (vertebrae,
ribs, sternum), the recorded abnormalities were duplica-
tions and anterior homeotic transformations (Table 5,
Fig. 2K,M,O).

To analyze the ectopic cartilage, D17 fetuses were
explanted from five females of control and FON

## DISCUSSION

The data obtained in the present study show that
FON is teratogenic in rats dosed at D9.5. The target
structures are those controlled by the morphogen RA
and the abnormalities observed are similar to those due

to exogenous RA exposure [cleft palate (Young et al.,
2000), cranio-facial malformations (Osumi-Yamashita,
1996), homeotic respecification of vertebral identity
(Kessel and Gruss, 1991; Kawanishi et al., 2003)] More-
over, the FON-related induction of maxillary ectopic
cartilage was confirmed in the present study. The ectopic
cartilage observed on maxilla was a cartilaginous
structure visible clearly at D17 as well. After FON 500
treatment the morphology of the ectopic cartilage was
linear, parallel to the mandibular Meckel's cartilage,
fused with the basal cartilages (appearing in FON fetuses
with an abnormal morphology). In the worst cases,
the ectopic cartilage was a large element joining the

During normal development, the maxillary component
of the first branchial arch gives rise to the dermal bones
of the maxilla and to the cartilaginous elements at the
base of the skull. By contrast, palatoquadrate and
Meckel's cartilage (usually considered maxillary and
mandibular cartilages) originate exclusively from the
mandibular prominence (Cerny et al., 2004). Regarding
the role of mesenchyme in organizing the maxillary and
mandibular buds, evidence shows that the ectomesench-
yme derived from encephalic NCC plays a role only
in the formation of the mandibular part of the
first branchial arch, whereas the maxillary component
exclusively originates from the post-optic mesoderm
(Lee et al., 2004).

Neural crest cell immunostaining at D10 and D11 of
FON-treated embryos indicates clearly a dose-related
alteration of NCC migration from the encephalon to
frontonasal and first branchial arch regions: migrating
NCC appear at D10 mixing and migrating in a
unique stream, with abnormal ectomesenchymal con-
densation originating at D11. In particular, at D11
the ectopic staining of the maxillary prominence is
evident if compared with the controls. This observation
leads to the hypothesis that an ectopic NCC condensa-
tion at the level of the maxillary bud could evolve
in cartilaginous fragmented elements (if the contribution
of NCC-derived ectomesenchyme is limited) or in
a skeletal extra element quite similar to the mandi-
bular Meckel's cartilage (if a conspicuous NCC migration
in the maxillary part of the branchial arch has
taken place). This hypothesis is also supported by the
observation that the ectopic maxillary cartilage is usually
fused with cartilaginous elements at the base of the skull,
suggesting a common anatomic origin of the two
structures.

The abnormal migration of NCC in FON groups is also
documented by the abnormal organization of the cranial
nerves (cranial nerves and ganglia have a double origin:
from NCC- derived ectomesenchyme and from placodes)
(Whitlock, 2004).

The maxillary ectopic cartilage has been described by
Morriss-Kay (1993) after treatment of pregnant mice with
teratogenic dosages of RA as a displacement of Meckel's
cartilage from the mandibular to the maxillary region.
The data obtained in the present work suggest a different
explanation to justify the ectopic maxillary cartilage: the
abnormal migration of part of NCC coded to condense at
the mandibular level to form cartilage gives rise to
ectopic ectomesenchyme that differentiates and forms
the ectopic cartilaginous bar.

In conclusion, this study shows that: 1) FON is
teratogenic in rats; 2) FON induces abnormalities similar
to those observed after RA treatment, including the
formation of the maxillary cartilage; and 3) the maxillary
cartilage originates by differentiation of the ectome-
senchyme condensed from the ectopically-migrated
encephalic NCC.
