### ABSTRACT

Background: Glyphosate is the most widely used herbicide globally. However, its association with frailty, an
emerging public health concern, and mortality in the general population remains unclear.

Methods: This cohort utilized the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) 2013-2018 data,
with follow-up through December 31, 2019. Weighted logistic regression, Cox regression, and restricted cubic
splines analyses were performed to investigate the association of urinary glyphosate with frailty, defined using a
49-item frailty index at baseline, and all-cause mortality, respectively. Causal mediation analysis and sensitivity
analysis were also employed.

Results: 4697 adults (mean age: 47.1 years; 48.9 % male) were included. Multivariable logistic regression showed
that elevated urinary glyphosate correlated with increased frailty prevalence (log2-transformed, adjusted OR =
1.14, 95 % CI: 1.04-1.25; quartiles, P for trend = 0.002), with a linear dose-response relationship (P value for
nonlinearity = 0.568). During a median follow-up of 3.9 years, 238 total deaths occurred. Multivariable-adjusted
Cox regression demonstrated that urinary glyphosate was positively related to all-cause mortality (log2-trans-
formed, adjusted HR = 1.19, 95 % CI: 1.07-1.31; quartiles, P for trend = 0.005). Additionally, frailty signifi-
cantly mediated the association between glyphosate and all-cause mortality, with mediation proportion of
30.3 %.

Conclusions: Urinary glyphosate was positively associated with frailty and all-cause mortality in the general US
adult population. Frailty also played a partial mediator role in this relationship. These findings provide new
evidence of adverse health effects of glyphosate and imply that enhanced efforts should be made to mitigate
exposure to glyphosate.


### 1. Introduction

Glyphosate is a broad-spectrum organophosphorus herbicide and the
most widely utilized herbicide in global agriculture, with its use
continuing to increase (Myers et al., 2016). Evidence reveals that the
majority of US general population have experienced recent exposure to
glyphosate, suggesting a widespread glyphosate exposure (Ospina et al.,
2022). Individuals can be exposed to glyphosate through various routes,
for example, dermal absorption, inhalation, and dietary intake (de
Morais Valentim et al., 2024). Previous animal studies, case reports, and
some observational studies have indicated that glyphosate exposure is

positively linked to nausea and vomiting (Luo et al., 2019), allergic
wheeze (Hoppin et al., 2017), hypothyroidism (Shrestha et al., 2018),
and miscarriages (Camacho and Mejia, 2017). Moreover, the carcino-
genicity of glyphosate has caused much controversy. In 2015, the In-
ternational Agency for Research on Cancer under the WHO re-classified
it as a "Group 2A" carcinogen, indicating it is probably carcinogenic to
humans (Guyton et al., 2015). Conversely, other researchers and regu-
latory agencies stated that glyphosate did not pose significant carcino-
genic threat to humans under appropriate use (Meftaul et al., 2020).
Given its widespread global use, further research involving large-scale
investigations is urgently needed to explore the relationship between

#### glyphosate and other human health outcomes.

Frailty is a multifaceted and complex clinical condition, which is
marked by multi-system functional declines and driven by inter-
connected biological and psychosocial vulnerabilities (Dent et al.,
2019). As the global elderly population continues to increase, frailty has
emerged as a substantial public health concern, posing a tremendous
challenge to human health. Large meta-analyses estimated that 17.4 %
of older adults in the community suffered from frailty (Siriwardhana
et al., 2018), with an overall incidence of 43.4 cases per 1000
person-years (Ofori-Asenso et al., 2019). Currently, the two predomi-
nant diagnostic models of frailty are the Fried's phenotypic criteria
(Fried et al., 2001) and the frailty index model that quantifies frailty
through a composite index incorporating psychosocial, cognitive, and
clinical domains (Searle et al., 2008). Abundant data and evidence have
shown that frailty is associated with hospitalizations and escalated
healthcare burdens, deteriorative quality of life, and elevated risk of
mortality (Hoogendijk et al., 2019). Nevertheless, the relationship be-
tween glyphosate exposure and frailty has not been elucidated. In
addition, most previous studies on glyphosate and death are based on
animal experiments, case reports (Cho et al., 2019), or case-control
studies with limited sample sizes (Moon et al., 2018). Therefore, to
achieve a more comprehensive understanding of the impact of glypho-
sate on mortality, large-scale prospective investigations in the general
population are warranted.

To address these research gaps, the present study aimed to examine
the association of urinary glyphosate levels with frailty, as defined by a
comprehensive frailty index method, as well as all-cause mortality in
adults, and to determine whether frailty played a mediator role, using
data from a nationally representative cohort from the National Health
and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES).


### 2. Methods


#### 2.1. Study design and participants

NHANES is a nationwide survey and employs a complex, multistage,
stratified probability sampling design to collect comprehensive health-
related characteristics from a representative sample of the non-
institutionalized population in the United States. Detailed information
regarding the survey's design, methodology, and estimation data is
available elsewhere (Johnson et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2020) and can

also be accessed at https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/nhanes/index.htm. The
protocols of NHANES were approved by the NCHS Research Ethics Re-
view Board, and all participants provided written informed consent.

Urinary glyphosate level was measured only in three NHANES cy-
cles: 2013-2014, 2015-2016, and 2017-2018. Thus, these 3 cycles were
utilized in this study. Of the 7067 participants with urinary glyphosate
data, 2328 were excluded due to the absence of frailty index or mortality
data (n = 2328). Further exclusions were made for participants without
urinary creatinine (UCr) value (n = 1) at baseline or those who were
pregnant (n = 41). Ultimately, the final analysis included a total of 4697
US adults (Fig. 1).


#### 2.2. Measurement of urinary glyphosate

In brief, according to NHANES methodology documents, urinary
glyphosate level was measured using the 2D-on-line IC-MS/MS tech-
nique (Schutze et al., 2021). Further details on the measurement process
of glyphosate are described elsewhere (Schutze et al., 2021;
Jauregui-Zunzunegui et al., 2024) and can be found at https://wwwn.
cdc.gov/Nchs/Nhanes/2013-2014/SSGLYP_H.htm. To account for
urine dilution, urinary glyphosate levels were corrected by UCr levels
and expressed as µg/g of UCr in this study (Ospina et al., 2022). Sub-
sequently, unweighted UCr-corrected glyphosate concentrations were
then divided into quartiles: quartile 1 (≤ 0.192, n = 1175), quartile 2
(0.192-0.326, n = 1177), quartile 3 (0.326-0.584, n = 1171), and
quartile 4 (> 0.584, n = 1174).


#### 2.3. Definitions of covariates

The covariates in this study were selected based on a combination of
prior glyphosate-related studies (Li et al., 2023; Han et al., 2024;
Untalan et al., 2024), accepted or commonly used risk factors associated
with frailty and mortality, and included common demographic charac-
teristics (participants' age, gender, race/ethnicity, education level, and
family income-to-poverty ratio [PIR]), medical history (encompassing
hypertension, diabetes, and hyperlipidemia), as well as lifestyle-related
behaviors (smoking status, alcohol consumption, and physical activity).
These data were collected through structured interviews, standardized
questionnaires, and physical/laboratory examinations.

#### 2.4. Ascertainment of frailty and mortality

Frailty at baseline was defined using the frailty index, as introduced
by Searle et al. (2008) and subsequently developed and validated in

other studies using data from NHANES (Hakeem et al., 2021; Chen et al.,
2019). Specifically, a 49-item frailty index across multiple health do-
mains was used, encompassing cognition problem (1 item), difficulties
with dependence (15 items), symptoms of depression (7 items),
self-reported multiple comorbidities (13 items), hospital utilization and
general health condition (5 items), BMI and handgrip strength (2 items),
and blood biomarkers (6 items) (Hakeem et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2019).
A detailed list of the frailty index items and their corresponding scores
are provided in Supplementary Table 1. Consistent with other studies,
frailty was defined as a frailty index greater than 0.21 (Hakeem et al.,
2021; Chen et al., 2019).

Information on mortality data for all participants were prospectively
gathered until December 31, 2019 and are publicly available (http
s://www.cdc.gov/nchs/data-linkage/mortality-public.htm).
The
outcome in this study was all-cause mortality. Follow-up time was
computed as the time period from the date of baseline urinary glypho-
sate measurement to either death or the end of follow-up, whichever
came first.


#### 2.5. Statistical analysis

Statistical analysis was conducted in accordance with NHANES
guidelines, with sample weights, stratification and clustering utilized in
all analyses to consider the complex sampling design of NHANES (Bao
et al., 2020). Specifically, the "svyset" command in Stata and the "sur-
vey" package in R software were used to account for the complex design
of NHANES. Stratification and clustering were directly provided in
NHANES source files, and NHANES also provided specific sample

weights for urinary glyphosate (variable names: WTSSCH2Y in NHANES
2013-2014, WTSSGL2Y in NHANES 2015-2016, and WTSSBJ2Y in
NHANES 2017-2018). According to NHANES analytic guidelines, when
integrating data across multiple survey cycles, the original sample
weights should be divided by the number of combined cycles, i.e., the
original weights for urinary glyphosate divided by 3 in the present study.

Continuous variables were reported as weighted mean with standard
error (SE), and categorical variables were shown as weighted percent-
ages with SE. Given the positively skewed distribution of urinary
glyphosate, a log2-transformation was applied (Han et al., 2024).

The associations of urinary glyphosate with frailty index (continuous
variable) and frailty (dichotomous variable) were investigated using
weighted linear and logistic regression, respectively. In addition, the
proportional hazards assumption for urinary glyphosate and all-cause
mortality was met (P = 0.113), as confirmed by Schoenfeld's re-
siduals. Weighted Kaplan-Meier curves were constructed to graph cu-
mulative all-cause mortality incidence on time scales of both follow-up
time and participants' age, and Cox regression was also employed.
Model 1 was the crude and unadjusted model. Model 2 was adjusted for
age, gender and race, which are recognized unmodifiable demographic
variables. Model 3 was adjusted for model 2 plus education level, PIR,
smoking status, alcohol drinking, history of hypertension, diabetes,
hyperlipidemia, physical activity, eGFR and BMI. For most of the
covariates in this study, the proportions of missing values were below
5 %, while for alcohol drinking, PIR and physical activity, the pro-
portions of missing values were 6.2 %, 9.4 % and 25.3 %, respectively
(Supplementary Table 2). Thus, to minimize the sample loss, multiple
imputation by chained equations was applied using the "mice" package
(Zhang, 2016). Furthermore, multivariable-adjusted restricted cubic
splines (RCS) using three default knots (10th, 50th, and 90th percen-
tiles) were also employed (Qiu et al., 2022).

Besides, a causal mediation analysis with multivariable adjustments
was conducted to investigate whether frailty could mediate the associ-
ation of urinary glyphosate with all-cause mortality. The mediation
proportion was calculated using the product method described by
VanderWeele based on counterfactual framework (Jaskulski et al., 2018;
VanderWeele, 2011). In brief, the total effect of urinary glyphosate on
all-cause mortality can be split into direct and indirect (through frailty)
effect. Among them, the natural direct effect (NDE) captures the direct
pathway from the exposure (urinary glyphosate) to the outcome (all --
cause death), independent of the mediator (frailty index), and the nat-
ural indirect effect (NIE) quantifies the effect of the exposure on the
outcome that acts through the mediator. The proportion of mediation
was calculated using the following formula: [HRNDE
x (HRNIE-1)]/(HRNDE × HRNIE-1) (Jaskulski et al., 2018; VanderWeele,
2011). In addition, sensitivity analyses were also performed. First, we
re-performed the statistical analysis using the original values without
multiple imputation to determine whether the imputation had a signif-
icant effect on the results. Second, urinary glyphosate was analyzed
without UCr correction, with UCr treated as a separate covariate in
multivariable regression models (Barr et al., 2005). Third, participants
who died within the first year of follow-up were excluded from the
analysis to reduce the risk of reverse causation bias (Qiu et al., 2022).

Stata software (16.0, StataCorp LLC, USA) and R software (4.2.3)
were used for all statistical analysis. A two-tailed P value less than 0.05
was considered statistically significant.

#### 3.4. Mediation effects of frailty index on the association between urinary glyphosate and mortality

The causal mediation analysis, illustrated in Fig. 4, indicated that
urinary glyphosate contributed to an elevated risk of all-cause death risk
partially through increasing the frailty index. Specifically, exposure to
glyphosate was correlated with a higher frailty index value, and per 0.1-
unit increment in frailty index was associated with a 77 % higher risk of
all-cause death (adjusted HR = 1.77, 95 % CI: 1.56-2.01) in the fully-
adjusted model. A significant 30.3 % of the total effect of urinary
glyphosate on all-cause mortality was through elevated frailty index and
remained consistent in model 1 (mediation proportion: 36.9 %) and
model 2 (33.9 %), implying a partial mediator role of frailty in this
association.


#### 3.5. Sensitivity analyses

In the first sensitivity analysis without data multiple imputation, the
positive associations between urinary glyphosate and frailty index,
frailty (Supplementary Table 3), and all-cause mortality (Supplementary
Table 4) did not change, suggesting that multiple imputation did not
significantly affect the results. The second sensitivity analysis examined
the associations of urinary glyphosate (ng/ml) without UCr correction
with frailty and mortality. Linear and logistic regression still

demonstrated positive associations between urinary glyphosate and
frailty index (P for trend < 0.001) and frailty prevalence (P for trend =
0.032) (Supplementary Table 5). Similarly, Cox regression still revealed
a positive glyphosate-all-cause mortality association (P for trend =
0.030) (Supplementary Table 6). In addition, when we excluded par-
ticipants who died within the first year of follow-up, their associations
(Supplementary Table 7 and Supplementary Table 8) remained
unchanged.


### 4. Discussion

In this sample of US adults, we examined the association of urinary
glyphosate level with frailty defined by a 49-item frailty index and all-
cause mortality and found that urinary glyphosate was positively asso-
ciated with frailty and mortality. Frailty also played a partial mediator
role in the relationship between urinary glyphosate and death.

To our knowledge, this is the first study exploring the association of
urinary glyphosate with frailty as well as all-cause mortality simulta-
neously in the US general population with the largest sample size to
date. Our findings add new and additional evidence of adverse health
effects of glyphosate exposure and, therefore, may have important
public health implications, which suggests that more regulatory actions
should be conducted to minimize glyphosate exposure.

Although glyphosate exposure and carcinogenic risk have been re-
ported with controversial conclusions (Guyton et al., 2015; Andreotti
et al., 2018), epidemiological studies on the effects of glyphosate
exposure on other health outcomes in humans are limited. Currently, no
existing epidemiological studies have explored the relationship between
glyphosate exposure and frailty, an increasingly important clinical
condition and public concern especially against the background of
global aging. The prevalence of frailty in the present study was 18.6 %,
which was similar to that in other studies conducted in community
settings (Siriwardhana et al., 2018). The lack of relevant epidemiolog-
ical research on glyphosate exposure and frailty makes direct compari-
sons of our findings with other studies challenging. Using a nationwide
cohort analysis, our study uncovered for the first time the adverse effects
of glyphosate on frailty. In addition, the positive association between
urinary glyphosate and death observed in this study was consistent with
previous case reports (Cho et al., 2019) and other studies (Moon et al.,
2018; Untalan et al., 2024), but our sample size was larger and weighted
statistics were used to produce more accurate estimates.

The exact biological mechanisms underlying the glyphosate expo-
sure and frailty relationship remain poorly understood, but several ex-
planations may involve. First, studies have identified glyphosate as a
contributor to reduced grip strength in adults (Jauregui-Zunzunegui
et al., 2024), and decreased grip strength is recognized to associate with
frailty. Second, studies in vitro and on animal models have revealed that
glyphosate can activate oxidative stress, DNA damage, inflammation,
lipid peroxidation, and also impair antioxidant activities (Wang et al.,
2022), while oxidative stress and frailty are intrinsically and closely
linked. Third, glyphosate exposure is associated with significant hor-
monal disturbance (Geier and Geier, 2023), such as reduced total
estradiol and testosterone, and there is evidence that such hormonal
changes positively contribute to frailty (Cawthon et al., 2009). Fourth,
glyphosate exposure has been linked to cognitive declines in older adults
(Ren et al., 2024). Since cognitive impairment is often associated with
frailty, this may serve as another pathway connecting glyphosate with
frailty and warrants further investigations.

Furthermore, our study also revealed a positive association between
frailty index and all-cause mortality, which was consistent with previous
abundant studies (Kojima et al., 2018). However, this study introduced a
mediating role of frailty index, shedding light on how glyphosate
exposure might influence mortality risk through its impact on frailty.

The present study has several strengths. First, this study is the first
and largest investigation to date to evaluate the relationship between
urinary glyphosate and frailty prevalence and all-cause mortality risk in

### 5. Conclusions

Urinary glyphosate was positively and linearly associated with frailty
and all-cause mortality in US general adults. Frailty emerged as a sig-
nificant mediator in the association between urinary glyphosate and all-
cause death, accounting for 30.3 % of the total effect. These findings
underscore the need for stricter measures and preventive strategies to
minimize exposure to glyphosate.
